Plastic Bottle (PET)

✓ Recyclable

Commonly used for beverages, this type of plastic is widely accepted in curbside bins.

Key Specifications

Category: Plastic
Resin Code: #1 (PET / PETE)
Common Uses: Water bottles, Juice bottles, Cooking oil containers

How to Recycle Properly

1 Empty the contents: Pour out any remaining liquids or food residue.
2 Rinse briefly: A quick swirl of water prevents sticky residue from contaminating other materials.
3 Crush the bottle: Squeeze the air out to save space in your bin and the collection truck.
4 Leave the cap ON: Screw the cap back on securely. Loose caps fall through sorting grates and end up in landfills.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • ✕ Leaving liquid inside: Heavy bottles can be mis-sorted by machines, and liquids ruin surrounding paper/cardboard.
  • ✕ Bagging your bottles: Never put recyclables in a plastic trash bag. Keep them loose in the bin.
  • ✕ Removing the label: You don't need to peel the label off; the recycling facility burns it off during processing.

Need a Drop-off Location?

Some municipalities require drop-offs for bulk plastics. Find a verified center near you.

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens after it gets recycled?

PET bottles are sorted, cleaned, and shredded into tiny flakes. These flakes are melted down and reshaped into new products like clothing fibers (fleece), carpets, strapping tape, or brand new plastic containers.

What is the environmental impact?

Recycling a single plastic bottle can save enough energy to power a 60-watt light bulb for up to 6 hours. It also keeps microplastics out of our oceans and reduces the need to extract raw petroleum to make new plastics.

Can I reuse these bottles instead?

While you can reuse them a few times, PET bottles are designed for single-use. Over time, heat and wear can cause bacteria to build up in tiny cracks. For long-term use, switch to a stainless steel or heavy-duty reusable plastic bottle.
